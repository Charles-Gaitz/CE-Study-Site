Chapter 1

The Demand for Auditing & Foundational Concepts

Why Do Audits Exist?

Before audits were required by law, 82% of NYSE firms voluntarily paid for them (1926 data). This tells you something important: the market demands audits because information asymmetry creates risk.

The Principal-Agent Problem Owners (stockholders = "principals") hire managers ("agents") to run the company. The agent has an information advantage and may use it to serve their own interests rather than the owners'. An independent auditor reduces this information risk.

The System of Investor Protection

Think of financial reporting as a multi-layer protection system. Each participant has a different combination of expertise and independence:

ParticipantRoleIndependenceExpertise
Management (CEO/CFO)Prepare and certify financial statements (SOX §302)LowHigh
Internal AuditorsTest controls and reporting; report to Audit CommitteeLow (employees)High
Audit CommitteeOversee financial reporting; group of independent directorsHighVaries
Board of DirectorsGovernance oversightModerate–HighVaries
External AuditorsProvide opinion on fairness of F/SHigh (required)High
Regulators (SEC, PCAOB)Set standards, enforce, inspectHighestHigh
Key Fact: EDGAR & MSFT Public company filings are available via the SEC's EDGAR tool. Microsoft paid Deloitte & Touche ~$53.88M in audit fees in 2025 on $281.7B in revenue — roughly 0.02%. Smaller companies pay a higher percentage.

Three Levels of Assurance

AssuranceLending of credibility by independent, objective intermediaries.
AttestationAssurance provided for specific assertions made by management. (e.g., SOC 1 reports)
AuditingWhen those assertions are embodied in a company's financial statements. Most specific form.

The AAA Definition of Auditing (1973)

Memorize This "A systematic process of objectively obtaining and evaluating evidence regarding assertions about economic actions and events to ascertain the degree of correspondence between those assertions and established criteria and communicating the results to interested users."

Management's Financial Statement Assertions (PCAOB)

Every audit procedure you perform is designed to test one or more of these five assertions. Know them cold.

AssertionWhat It MeansExample Risk
Existence/OccurrenceRecorded assets/transactions actually exist and occurredFictitious sales; phantom inventory
CompletenessAll transactions and balances are recordedUnrecorded liabilities; omitted sales
Valuation/AllocationAssets, liabilities, and transactions valued per GAAPWrong credit rating → bad AR valuation
Rights & ObligationsEntity has legal claim to assets and responsibility for liabilitiesAssets leased but recorded as owned

To find existence violations → sample FROM the report (do the items in the report actually exist?). To find completeness violations → sample FROM the truth/reality (are all real things captured in the report?)

Demand for Audits Increases With…

  • Environmental Risk: Complexity, Remoteness, Time-sensitivity, High consequences
  • Information Risk: Higher probability that information is false or misleading

SOC Reports

SOC 1 — Controls at a service organization relevant to user entities' ICFR. Very common attestation. Covers controls of outsourced environments (e.g., ADP for payroll). ADP gets audited by PwC; Exxon uses ADP — PwC's SOC 1 gives Exxon (and its auditors) comfort over those controls.

System of Quality Control — 6 Elements

  1. Leadership responsibilities ("tone at the top")
  2. Relevant ethical requirements
  3. Acceptance and continuance of client relationships
  4. Human resources
  5. Engagement performance
  6. Monitoring

GAAS — Three Fundamental Principles

1. Responsibilities

  • Competence & Capabilities — Education, experience, expertise
  • Independence — In fact (objective mindset) AND in appearance (perceptions)
  • Due Care — Level of a "prudent auditor" in similar circumstances
  • Professional Skepticism — Questioning mindset; inquiry alone is NEVER enough
  • Professional Judgment — Used throughout; not just rule-following
Professional skepticism = critically assessing verbal evidence from management. It is NOT just relying on external evidence or focusing only on material items (those relate to judgment and materiality).

2. Performance

  • Goal: Reasonable assurance F/S are free of material misstatement
  • Plan and supervise; assess materiality; assess risk
  • Obtain sufficient (quantity) and appropriate (quality = relevant + reliable) evidence
Sufficiency vs. Appropriateness Sufficiency = quantity. Higher risk → need more evidence. Higher quality evidence often means you need less of it.
Appropriateness = quality (relevance + reliability). More reliable sources: independent external parties, auditor's own direct knowledge, original documents.
Most reliable audit evidence = inspection of bank statements obtained directly from the client's financial institution (external, original source).

3. Reporting

  • Express an opinion (or disclaim) on the F/S
  • Assess against a financial reporting framework (GAAP, IFRS)

The Audit Report — Your "Product"

  • Expresses an opinion on the financial statements
  • Provides reasonable assurance (high level, not absolute)
  • Conducted in accordance with PCAOB or AICPA standards
  • Since 2004: Large public companies also get an opinion on ICFR

Types of Opinions (Quick Reference)

Opinion TypeMeaning
Unmodified/Unqualified"Clean" — F/S conform with GAAP. No issues.
Qualified"Except for" limited items, F/S conform with GAAP
AdverseF/S do NOT conform with GAAP (material, pervasive misstatement)
DisclaimerAuditor cannot express an opinion (scope limitation)
Chapter 3

Pre-Engagement, Planning, Materiality & Audit Documentation

Pre-Engagement Activities

1. Communication with Predecessor Auditors

When a new firm takes on a client, they must attempt to communicate with the predecessor auditor. If the client permits communication, topics include:

  • Management's integrity
  • Disagreements about accounting principles or audit procedures
  • Communications about fraud, illegal acts, or internal control recommendations
  • Why the auditor change happened (especially if the predecessor was fired)

2. Compliance with Independence Requirements

Auditors must be independent in fact (objective) and in appearance (no perception of bias). Failure can lead to disciplinary action and litigation.

3. Engagement Letter

A formal contract outlining responsibilities of both the client and the auditor. Protects both parties. Based on AS 1301.

The Audit Plan

The auditor develops and documents a plan describing procedures to assess the risk of material misstatement at the financial statement and assertion level. The plan determines the nature, timing, and extent of tests.

Staffing

  • Engagement partner, Audit manager, Audit staff, IT specialist, Tax partner, QA partner
  • More experienced staff → new clients, complex transactions, public clients

Using Internal Auditors

  • Must assess their objectivity and competence first
  • Rely less in risky, judgmental areas
  • Can be used via reliance (you rely on their work) or direct assistance (they work as part of your team)

Using Specialists

  • Auditor-employed specialists (actuaries, geologists): must be unrelated to the company; not typically mentioned in audit report
  • Company-employed specialists: must evaluate their objectivity, competence, methods, and assumptions

Materiality — The Three Steps

Materiality = an amount or event that would influence the decisions of a reasonable user. Requires professional judgment.

U.S. Supreme Court Definition A fact is material if there is "a substantial likelihood that the fact would have been viewed by the reasonable investor as having significantly altered the 'total mix' of information made available."

Step 1 — Determine Overall (Planning) Materiality

Typically 5% of income before taxes (IBT) for public companies. Common bases: IBT, total assets, net assets, total revenues, gross profit, total equity. Adjust lower for qualitative factors (prior misstatements, fraud risk, loan covenant pressure, volatile environment).

Step 2 — Determine Tolerable Misstatement (Performance Materiality)

Allocated to individual accounts. Typically 50–75% of overall materiality. Used to design specific tests.

Step 3 — Evaluate Audit Findings

  • Aggregate all identified misstatements (known + likely), except "clearly trivial" ones
  • Clearly trivial = ~3–5% of overall materiality (you don't even need to tell the client)
  • Compare aggregate to overall materiality; account-level to tolerable misstatement
  • Always communicate uncorrected misstatements to the audit committee (PCAOB AS1301)
  • Also evaluate qualitatively (e.g., does it change a loss to income? Does it affect loan covenants?)
Enron Example Arthur Andersen decided NOT to require correction of a $51M error that would have cut Enron's 1997 income from $105M to $54M. They justified it using "normalized" income of prior years ($584M, $520M, $453M). This is a cautionary tale in materiality judgment.
Overall Materiality Example: $1B IBT × 5% = $50M
Tolerable Misstatement: $50M × 50% = $25M (per account)
Clearly Trivial: $50M × 3% = $1.5M (don't even post to SAM)

Audit Procedures — Three Purposes

  1. Risk Assessment Procedures — understand client and risks
  2. Tests of Controls — test operating effectiveness of internal controls
  3. Substantive Procedures — evidence about management's assertions

Audit Documentation Requirements

Documentation = written record of the basis for the auditor's conclusions. Must be sufficient so that an experienced auditor with no prior connection can understand the work performed.

  • Must show nature, timing, extent, and results of procedures
  • Must document who did the work and when
  • Must list significant accounts and relevant assertions
  • Retention: 7 years from report release date (or last day of fieldwork if no report)
  • Finalization: All documentation finalized within 45 days of audit report release
Chapter 4

Audit Risk Model & Risk Assessment

The Audit Risk Model

Audit Risk (AR) = probability that the auditor gives an unqualified opinion on materially misstated financial statements. Applied at the assertion level.

AR = IR × CR × DR
ComponentDefinitionWho Controls It
Inherent Risk (IR)Probability of material misstatement ignoring controls. Function of the client's business, transaction types, management integrity.The client (auditor only assesses)
Control Risk (CR)Probability that the client's internal controls fail to prevent or detect a material misstatement.The client (auditor only assesses)
Detection Risk (DR)Probability that the auditor's substantive procedures fail to detect a material misstatement that exists.The AUDITOR controls this
DR = AR ÷ (IR × CR)    ← RMM = IR × CR
Critical Insight IR and CR are properties of the client — the auditor cannot change them, only assess them. The auditor's ONLY lever is Detection Risk, which is controlled by adjusting the nature, timing, and extent of substantive procedures.

How DR Affects Audit Work

Lower Detection Risk (more work)Higher Detection Risk (less work)
NatureMore effective testsLess effective tests
TimingMore testing at year-endMore testing at interim
ExtentMore tests / larger samplesFewer tests
If IR ↑ or CR ↑ → RMM ↑ → DR must ↓ → Auditor does MORE work.
If IR ↓ or CR ↓ → RMM ↓ → DR can ↑ → Auditor can do LESS work.

Limitations of the Audit Risk Model

  • Subject to judgments used as inputs
  • Doesn't account for potential auditor error
  • Cannot assess RMM = 0 (can never have zero risk of material misstatement)
  • Virtually impossible to calculate exact levels — it's conceptual

Fraud Risk

The difference between fraud and error is intent. The auditor must obtain reasonable assurance that F/S are free from material misstatement whether caused by fraud or error.

Fraudulent F/S ReportingIntentional misstatements/omissions to deceive users. Management fraud.
Misappropriation of AssetsAsset theft. More common but often less material.

Fraud Triangle

  • Incentive/Pressure: Analyst pressure, personal financial problems
  • Opportunity: Weak internal controls, complex org structure
  • Rationalization/Attitude: Management overrides accounting; history of securities violations
Communication of Fraud Fraud evidence must go to at least one level above the people involved. Any fraud by management (regardless of amount) is material and must be reported to the audit committee. Exercise great care — accusations of fraud are taken very seriously.

Risk Assessment Process (Understanding the Client)

Before assessing risk, auditors must understand:

  • Industry, regulatory, and external factors
  • Nature of the company and related parties
  • Effect of computerized processing
  • Accounting principles and disclosures
  • Objectives, strategies, and business risks
  • Performance measures and analysis

Required Procedures in Risk Assessment

  • Preliminary Analytical Procedures: Identify potential problems; familiarize with client
  • Brainstorming (Audit Team Discussion): Required. Understand how fraud might be perpetrated; discuss prior experiences; set the right tone
  • Inquiries of Management, Audit Committee, and Internal Auditors: Ask about accounting principles, susceptibility to errors and fraud, how they monitor fraud risks

Purpose of Inherent Risk Assessment

Determine significant accounts and relevant assertions, and how each could be misstated. An account is significant if there is a reasonable possibility it could contain a material misstatement (AS 2110.59e).

Noncompliance with Laws and Regulations

Direct-EffectProduces direct, material effects on F/S (e.g., income tax evasion). Auditor must design procedures to provide reasonable assurance.
Indirect-EffectNot tied to specific F/S accounts (e.g., insider trading, OSHA violations). Auditor follows up only on suspected violations that are material.

Ethics, Independence & the AICPA Code of Conduct

What is an Ethical Problem?

A situation where you must choose among alternatives and the right choice is unclear — specifically where your choice affects the well-being of others. Often a conflict between what you should do vs. what you want to do.

Ethical frameworks include: Utilitarianism (consequences), Deontological/Imperative (rules), and Virtue Ethics (consistency with your ideal self).

AICPA Code of Professional Conduct — Structure

LevelDescription
PrinciplesIdeal standards (aspirational goals): Responsibilities, Public Interest, Integrity, Objectivity & Independence, Due Care, Scope of Services
Rules of ConductMinimum standards — specific, enforceable rules
InterpretationsAICPA ethics division interpretations of the rules
Ethical RulingsPublished Q&A explanations about specific situations
Who Must Follow the AICPA Code? All CPAs — including those auditing public companies. Why? The SEC requires the signing auditor for a public company to be a CPA, and courts hold CPAs to the Code's standards.

The Key Rules — Know These

Rule 1.200 — Independence

Members in public practice must be independent when performing professional services. Two dimensions:

  • Independence in fact: Objective mental state, lack of bias
  • Independence in appearance: Would a reasonable investor, knowing all facts, conclude the auditor is objective and impartial?

7 Threats to Independence

  1. Familiarity — Long-standing or close relationship with client
  2. Adverse Interest — Auditor acting in opposition to client (e.g., litigation)
  3. Undue Influence — Client trying to coerce or give significant gifts
  4. Self-Review — Reviewing your own prior work
  5. Financial Self-Interest — Financial relationship with the client
  6. Management Participation — Taking on management's role
  7. Advocacy — Promoting client's position or interests

Who is a "Covered Member"?

  • All individuals participating in the engagement
  • Individual in a position to influence the engagement
  • Partner or manager who provides non-attest services to the client
  • Partner in the office where the engagement partner practices
  • The firm's benefit plan
  • Any entity controlled by any of the above

Financial Independence Rules

  • Covered member cannot have ANY direct financial interest in the client (even $1 stock)
  • Cannot have a material indirect financial interest
  • Spouse/spousal equivalent/dependents are treated the same as the covered member
  • Close relatives (parents, siblings, non-dependent children) can have some investment unless they are in a management position at the client
  • Threatened litigation from MANAGEMENT impairs independence; lawsuits from 3rd parties do not

SEC/PCAOB Independence — Additional Rules

  • Partners limited to 5 consecutive years on an engagement
  • 1-year "cooling off" period for employees who go to work in a financial reporting oversight role at a former audit client
  • Firm not independent if audit partner's compensation is based on selling non-audit services to that client

Prohibited Non-Audit Services for Audit Clients (SEC/PCAOB)

The auditor cannot also do: bookkeeping, financial info systems design, appraisals, actuarial services, internal audit, management functions, HR, broker-dealer services, legal services, expert services, contingent fee services, aggressive tax planning, or tax services for key executives.

Rule 1.100 — Integrity & Objectivity "If it seems bad, it is bad." You cannot comply with every bureaucratic rule but still lack independence. You must be free of conflicts of interest and not subordinate your judgment to others. You must not knowingly misrepresent facts.

Other Key Rules (Brief)

Rule 1.300–1.320Competence: Only perform services you can complete with professional competence; exercise due care; plan and supervise all engagements.
Rule 1.400Acts Discreditable: Cannot make false JEs, fail regulatory requirements, fail to file personal tax return, disclose CPA exam questions.
Rule 1.500Fees: Contingent fees NOT permitted for attest clients; allowed for non-attest in some cases. Commissions: permitted for non-attest if disclosed; prohibited for attest clients. Referral fees always allowed if disclosed.
Rule 1.600Advertising: Permitted, but cannot be false/misleading, cannot claim ability to influence 3rd parties, cannot underestimate fees.
Rule 1.700Confidentiality: Cannot disclose client info without consent. Exceptions: court subpoena, PCAOB inspection/peer review, ethics investigation.
Rule 1.800Form & Name: Any legal form (LLP, LLC). Name not misleading. Partners in firm name must be CPAs.

Regulatory & Disciplinary Bodies

  • Self-regulation: AICPA, State Societies of CPAs
  • Public regulation: State Boards of Accountancy, SEC, PCAOB, IRS
Ethical Challenges in Auditing Most additional audit effort changes nothing. But it's your professional responsibility to audit diligently even if you blow the time budget. Human nature is to be friendly and liked — but effective auditing requires professional skepticism and sometimes confrontation. That's the job.
Chapter 6

Internal Control (COSO) & Integrated Audits (SOX 404)

COSO Definition of Internal Control

COSO Definition "Internal control is a process, effected by an entity's board of directors, management and other personnel, designed to provide reasonable assurance regarding the achievement of objectives relating to operations, reporting, and compliance."

COSO is not required by law, but the SEC recognizes it as a suitable framework for evaluating ICFR effectiveness.

5 COSO Components

1. Control Environment (The Foundation)

Sets the "tone at the top." Board & senior management influence control consciousness throughout the organization. Principles: commitment to ethics, board oversight, proper authority structures, competent people, accountability.

2. Risk Assessment (Management's Risk)

Management's identification and analysis of risks related to achieving objectives. This is NOT the same as the auditor's risk assessment. Includes identifying potential fraud risks. Principles: clear objectives, identify and analyze risks, consider fraud, identify changes that impact control.

3. Control Activities

Policies and procedures that ensure management directives are carried out. Examples:

  • Segregation of Duties — separate authorization, physical custody, and recording
  • Approval and co-signing requirements
  • Prenumbered sequence controls and documentation trails
  • Restricted physical access
  • Reconciliations and independent cross-checks
Control ActivityAssertion Supported
Purchase orders must be authorized before purchaseExistence/Occurrence
Invoices matched to receiving report and PO (3-way match)Valuation/Accuracy
Prenumbered documents accounted forCompleteness
Checks not printed until voucher packet preparedExistence
Employee compares printed check amounts to voucher packetValuation
Only authorized signers sign checksExistence

4. Information and Communication

Controls over how the organization communicates internally and externally. Quality of information used within communication matters. Auditors cannot blindly rely on information produced by the company's own systems.

5. Monitoring

Management's ongoing assessment of internal control quality. Includes internal audit reviews, supervisory review, follow-up on errors, audit committee inquiries. Essentially "controls over controls."

Limitations of Internal Control

  • Human error
  • Collusion — two people bypass segregation of duties
  • Management override — management bypasses controls they created
  • Cost/benefit: Cost of a control may exceed its benefit. Hence only "reasonable" assurance, not absolute.

Auditor Strategies After Understanding Internal Control

Substantive StrategySet control risk HIGH (controls are irrelevant, ineffective, or inefficient to test). Perform more substantive procedures. No reliance on controls.
Reliance StrategyPlan to rely on controls → control risk is LOWER → detection risk can be HIGHER → fewer substantive procedures needed. BUT you must first test the control to validate it's designed and operating effectively.

Integrated Audits & SOX 404

An integrated audit = auditor gives an opinion on both the F/S AND the effectiveness of ICFR.

SOX SectionWho It Applies ToWhat's Required
404aALL issuers (public companies)Management reports on ICFR effectiveness; accept responsibility; evaluate using a framework; support with evidence
404bAccelerated filers only (>$75M market cap)External auditor independently assesses and reports on ICFR effectiveness (integrated audit)

Accelerated Filer Filing Deadlines

  • Large accelerated filer (>$700M): 60 days after year-end
  • Accelerated filer (>$75M): 75 days after year-end
  • Non-accelerated filer (<$75M): 90 days after year-end

ICFR Deficiency Levels

LevelDefinitionRequired Action
DeficiencyDesign or operation of control doesn't allow prevention/detection of misstatements on a timely basisDiscuss with management
Significant DeficiencyLess severe than MW, but merits attention by those overseeing F/RReport to audit committee
Material Weakness (MW)Reasonable possibility that a material misstatement will NOT be prevented or detected on a timely basisAdverse ICFR opinion (integrated audit); report to audit committee; management must also disclose
Critical Points on MW vs. Misstatements A material weakness in ICFR does NOT automatically mean there IS a material misstatement — it means there's a reasonable possibility one could exist. BUT if you find an actual material misstatement, that almost always implies a material weakness in ICFR (because the control failed to catch it).

Factors Distinguishing MW from Significant Deficiency

  • Does it involve the control environment?
  • Does it repeat regularly?
  • What is the magnitude?
  • Is the process complex and subjective?
  • Are there compensating controls?
  • Did it result in an actual material error?

Remediation

ICFR opinions are as of the fiscal year-end. If a MW is found during interim, the client can fix ("remediate") it before year-end. But remediation must be completed AND tested before the balance sheet date.

Types of ICFR Reports

  • Unqualified: Controls designed and operating effectively (no MW)
  • Adverse: Required if a material weakness is identified
  • Disclaimer: Required if there's a serious scope limitation
  • No "Qualified" Opinion in integrated audits — once you find a MW, it's adverse
Can a Company Get a Clean F/S Opinion but Adverse ICFR Opinion? YES. Peloton's June 30, 2022 10-K is an example. The F/S could be fairly stated even with weak controls if the misstatements are caught and corrected.

SOC 1 Reports (Service Organizations)

Type 1Covers the DESIGN of ICFR at the service organization (as of a point in time)
Type 2Covers DESIGN and OPERATING EFFECTIVENESS of ICFR (over a period of time) — more useful
Chapter 7

Substantive Procedures: Cash & Revenue Cycles

The Cash Cycle

Cash is relevant to many accounting cycles. It's highly liquid, easily transportable, and not easily identifiable — making it high inherent risk.

Key Documents

  • Cash Receipts Journal (debits to cash)
  • Cash Disbursements Journal (credits to cash)
  • Bank Reconciliation (reconciles GL cash vs. bank balance)
  • Bank Statements & Cancelled Checks

Relevant Assertions for Cash

Existence, Valuation, Presentation & Disclosure (cash is needed to pay debts, is highly liquid, and easily misappropriated)

Cash Receipts Process Flow

  1. Receive cash and remittance advice in mail
  2. Two employees open mail; prepare remittance listing
  3. Enter total from remittance listing in cash receipts journal
  4. Prepare deposit slip; deposit cash daily (intact)
  5. Update subsidiary AR using remittance advice
  6. Reconcile remittance listing, subsidiary AR, and deposit slip daily
Key Segregation of Duties Issue The person who receives cash should NOT also post to the AR subsidiary ledger. This would create an opportunity to pocket cash and cover it up by not recording the receipt.

Cash Disbursements Process

  • Voucher packet (purchase req + PO + receiving report + invoice) matched before authorization
  • Vouchers marked PAID after processing
  • Debit to AP and credit to cash must be done by someone WITHOUT access to check-writing

Auditing Cash — Existence Assertion

Risk: Cash balance doesn't exist in bank accounts
Control: CFO performs detailed review of bank reconciliation monthly
Test of Control: Randomly select 3 months; look for evidence of CFO's review
Substantive Test: Randomly select 3 months; independently reperform the bank reconciliation

How to Reperform a Bank Reconciliation

Line ItemKey AssertionProcedure
Balance per Bank—Confirm directly with bank
+ Deposits in TransitExistenceVouch to cutoff bank statement (did bank receive them?); Trace from cash receipts journal
− Outstanding ChecksCompletenessTrace checks cleared from cutoff bank statement; Vouch to cash disbursements journal
± Debit/Credit Memos—Inspect bank memo; examine supporting documentation
Balance per Books—Foot reconciliation; trace to trial balance

Cutoff bank statement = bank statement for the short period right after year-end (e.g., first 10 days of January for a Dec 31 year-end). Used to verify deposits in transit did land at the bank and outstanding checks did clear.

Auditing Cash — Valuation Assertion

Risk: Foreign currency cash not translated properly
Control: Treasurer reviews cash translation adjustment monthly; checks spot rates
Test of Control: Randomly select 3 months; look for evidence of treasurer's review
Substantive Test: Randomly select 3 months; match each foreign currency spot rate to third-party pricing services

Confirmation of Bank Balances

  • Standard bank confirmations (paper) are increasingly rare
  • Most banks now use electronic confirmation via third-party intermediaries
  • Must be mailed/sent under auditor's control (not through the client)
  • Confirms deposit balances AND loan balances (existence and completeness)

The Revenue Cycle

Assuming a manufacturer that sells on credit and recognizes revenue upon shipment.

Key Documents

  • Customer Sales Order, Credit Approval Form, Open-Order Report
  • Shipping Document (Bill of Lading — third-party evidence!)
  • Sales Invoice, Sales Journal
  • Customer Statement, AR Subsidiary Ledger, Aged Trial Balance of AR
  • Remittance Advice, Cash Receipts Journal
  • Credit Memorandum (returns), Write-Off Authorization

Inherent Risks in Revenue

  • Inflating revenue to increase profitability
  • Fictitious sales (channel stuffing, bill-and-hold schemes)
  • Returns and allowances
  • Collectability of receivables

Relevant Assertions

AccountRelevant Assertions
RevenueOccurrence, Completeness, Cutoff
Accounts ReceivableExistence, Completeness, Valuation

Revenue Cycle — Assertion by Assertion

Revenue Occurrence (Did these sales actually happen?)

  • Risk: Fictitious sales added to inflate revenue
  • Control: System requires shipping document before recording revenue
  • Substantive Test: Sample sales from sales detail file → vouch back to shipping documents, invoice, customer order (VOUCHING = existence)

Revenue Completeness (Were all sales recorded?)

  • Risk: Sales made but not recorded
  • Control: System records revenue upon receiving shipping document
  • Substantive Test: Sample shipping documents → trace forward to sales journal (TRACING = completeness)

AR Existence (Do these receivable balances actually exist?)

  • Substantive Test: Confirm with customers (send confirmation letters)

AR Valuation (Are receivables collectible?)

  • Risk: Uncollectible portion not properly estimated
  • Substantive Tests: Compare write-offs to ADA estimate; inspect evidence of collectability for old accounts; recalculate allowance and bad debt expense

AR Confirmations — The Details

TypeHow It WorksWhen to Use
PositiveAsk recipient to confirm whether balance is correct OR incorrect; response required either waySmall number of accounts; large balances; expecting errors
Blank PositivePositive confirmation but don't tell them the balance — they fill it inWhen recipient might rubber-stamp a number without checking
NegativeOnly respond if something is wrong; silence = agreementLow risk, large number of small balances, customers expected to respond properly
Confirmation Considerations • Positive > Negative for reliability
• Recipients might not report understatements (→ confirmations better for existence than completeness)
• Be skeptical of unexpected response methods (fax, email instead of mail)
• Non-responses to positive: follow up; lower-than-expected response rate = red flag for fictitious accounts
• Common strategy: positive for large balances, negative for a sample of smaller accounts

Alternative Procedures (When Confirmation Not Available)

  • Vouch to subsequent cash collections (usually sufficient for existence and valuation)
  • Examine shipping documents (Bill of Lading = third-party evidence)
  • Examine sales orders, invoices, shipping documents
  • Inspect correspondence files for past-due accounts

Audit Sampling: Attributes & Variables

Why Sample?

Testing 100% is cost prohibitive. Sampling trades some effectiveness for efficiency. Used when the number of items is large and exact information is less critical than a reasonable estimate.

Two Approaches

Statistical SamplingUses probability in both selection and evaluation. Allows team to quantify and control sampling risk. More costly to design but more defensible.
Nonstatistical SamplingNo probability; auditor uses judgment for size and evaluation. Cannot mathematically quantify sampling risk.
For statistical sampling, you can ONLY use unrestricted random selection or systematic random selection (not block or haphazard).

Types of Sample Selection

  • Unrestricted Random: Items selected based on random numbers matched to population
  • Systematic Random: Random start, then every nth item (e.g., every 50th invoice)
  • Block: Contiguous units (e.g., all December transactions) — not for statistical sampling
  • Haphazard: Nonsystematic; humans are bad at being random — not for statistical sampling

Sampling Risk — The Two Types to Know

In Tests of ControlsIn Substantive TestingConsequence
Type I (Incorrect Rejection / Underreliance)Sample says control is NOT working; it actually ISSample says balance IS misstated; it actually is NOTEfficiency loss — you do more work than needed
Type II (Incorrect Acceptance / Overreliance)Sample says control IS working; it actually is NOTSample says balance is NOT misstated; it actually ISEffectiveness loss — you miss a real problem!
Type II Errors Are the Dangerous Ones Incorrect acceptance (overreliance) = audit effectiveness problem. You issue a clean opinion on misstated statements. Type I errors just waste time (efficiency problem).

Attributes Sampling (Tests of Controls)

Used to estimate the rate at which a control is NOT functioning (deviation rate). Goal: determine if the control is reliable enough to rely on.

Key Terms

Tolerable Rate of Deviation (TRD)Maximum deviation rate auditor is willing to accept and still rely on the control
Expected Population Deviation Rate (EPDR)Estimated deviation rate based on client history or prior audits
Sample Rate of Deviation (SRD)Actual deviations found ÷ sample size
Upper Limit Rate of Deviation (ULRD)SRD + allowance for sampling risk. This is what you compare to TRD.

Decision Rule

ULRD ≤ TRD → Rely on controls as planned
ULRD > TRD → Reduce planned reliance on controls (do more substantive work)

Sample Size for Attributes (How to Read the Table)

  1. Select table for desired risk of overreliance (e.g., 5%)
  2. Find row for expected population deviation rate (e.g., 1%)
  3. Find column for tolerable rate of deviation (e.g., 15%)
  4. Read sample size at the intersection

ULRD Calculation (How to Read the Evaluation Table)

  1. Select table for desired risk of overreliance (e.g., 5%)
  2. Find row for actual sample size (e.g., 30)
  3. Find column for actual number of deviations found (e.g., 3)
  4. Read ULRD at intersection (e.g., 23.9%)

Then: ULRD (23.9%) vs. TRD (e.g., 15%) → 23.9 > 15 → reduce reliance on controls.

Allowance for sampling risk = ULRD − SRD (e.g., 23.9% − 10% = 13.9%)

Qualitative Considerations Beyond just counting deviations, consider: Are they pervasive or isolated? Intentional or unintentional? Misunderstanding vs. carelessness? These qualitative factors may lead you to reduce reliance even if ULRD ≤ TRD.

Variables Sampling (Substantive Tests)

Used to estimate the dollar amount (value) of a population. Goal: determine if an account balance is materially misstated.

Key Terms

Tolerable Misstatement (TM)Maximum misstatement auditor will accept (from materiality analysis)
Actual Misstatement (AM)True misstatement in the population (unknown; estimated from sample)
Upper Limit on Misstatement (ULM)Estimated misstatement + allowance for sampling risk. This is compared to TM.

Decision Rule

ULM ≤ TM → Account is not materially misstated
ULM > TM → Account is materially misstated

Completing the Audit: Subsequent Events, Going Concern & Representations

The Audit Timeline

PeriodWhat Happens
Beginning of year → Year-endInterim testing (tests of controls + substantive procedures)
Year-end → Date of auditor's reportComplete substantive procedures; attorney letters; rep letter; going concern; adjusting entries; documentation review; subsequent events
Report date → Report release dateSubsequently discovered facts
After report releaseSubsequently discovered facts; omitted procedures

Completing Substantive Procedures

  • Roll-forward procedures: Extend interim work to the balance sheet date
  • Final analytical procedures (required): Overall review; investigate unexpected balances; review "miscellaneous," "other," and "clearing" accounts (potential earnings management)
  • Accounting estimates review: Review management's process; assess reasonableness of assumptions (e.g., ADA, asset impairments, depreciation lives)

Attorney's Letters

Auditors initiate → Client prepares description of litigation/claims/assessments → Auditors send to attorney → Attorney responds directly to auditors.

Attorney confirms or corrects the client's description of pending legal matters. This is critical for identifying contingent liabilities.

Written Representations (Rep Letter)

  • Provided by management to auditors
  • Dated using the date of the auditor's report
  • Purposes: (1) Impress on management its primary responsibility for F/S; (2) Auditor defense if questions arise later
  • If management refuses to provide: Typically withdraw or disclaim; can also qualify the opinion

Going-Concern Assessment

Auditors must consider whether evidence raises questions about the entity's ability to continue operating for a "reasonable period of time."

  • If no concerns remain after reviewing management's plans → No effect on report
  • If concerns remain → Management should use going concern accounting; auditors modify report
  • Options: Add explanatory paragraph (still unqualified) or disclaimer (rare, if can't value assets)
  • If entity doesn't properly disclose: GAAP departure → qualified or adverse opinion

Note: About 17% of going-concern opinions are issued by Big 4; only ~3% of Big 4 audits result in going-concern opinions. Small auditors wonder if they should issue them even when needed.

Adjusting Entries

  • Accumulate dollar effects of all identified misstatements
  • Evaluate materiality (quantitative and qualitative)
  • Recommend adjustment of ALL misstatements identified — but it's management's decision whether to adjust
  • Carry uncorrected misstatements forward for future audit consideration
  • Communicate all adjustments to audit committee/governance

Subsequent Events (During Audit)

Events occurring between the balance sheet date and the date of the auditor's report.

TypeDescriptionWhat To Do
Type IAdditional evidence of conditions that EXISTED at the balance sheet date (just clarified after)Adjust the financial statements
Type IIConditions that did NOT exist at the balance sheet date — new eventsDisclose in footnotes; possibly pro forma F/S; do NOT adjust
Type I Example: Settlement of a lawsuit for $100K that was estimated at $12K in the Dec 31 F/S → ADJUST the F/S (condition existed at year-end; new info about existing situation).

Type II Example: Stock issuance on Jan 30; building destroyed by storm on March 1 → DISCLOSE only (new event, did not exist at Dec 31).

Subsequently Discovered Facts (After Report Issued)

Facts learned AFTER the report date that, if known earlier, may have caused a different opinion.

  • If discovered between report date and release date:
    Option A: Revise date of report to new completion date (extends auditor liability)
    Option B: Dual-date the report (limits liability to specific new item)
  • If discovered after report release: If facts would result in revision of report AND individuals are relying on F/S → Notify those individuals

Omitted Procedures

Auditor realizes a procedure wasn't performed that should have been. Perform the omitted procedure if:

  1. The omission impairs ability to support the previously expressed opinion, AND
  2. Individuals are currently relying on the F/S and auditor's report

If opinion can still be supported → no action needed. If not → withdraw original report and issue revised reports.

Chapter 10

Audit Reports & Types of Opinions

The Four Types of Reports

Report TypeWhen Used
1. Unqualified / Unmodified (Clean)F/S conform with GAAP. No issues.
2. Unqualified with Explanatory ParagraphF/S conform with GAAP but additional matters disclosed (going concern, consistency change, CAMs, ICFR reference)
3. Qualified ("Except For")Scope limitation or GAAP departure that is material but NOT pervasive
4. AdverseGAAP departure that is material AND pervasive
5. DisclaimerScope limitation that is material AND pervasive; auditor cannot express an opinion

Decision Trees for Modified Opinions

Scope Limitations (Auditor Can't Get Enough Evidence)

  • Not material → Standard report (clean)
  • Material but NOT pervasive + alternative procedures available → Standard report
  • Material but NOT pervasive + no alternative procedures → Qualified opinion
  • Material AND pervasive → Disclaimer of opinion
Circumstance-ImposedBeyond auditor's/client's control (e.g., natural disaster prevents inventory observation; late engagement appointment)
Client-ImposedClient restricts procedures. Treat as significant restriction → disclaimer is ordinarily issued. Essentially always pervasive.

Departures from GAAP (F/S Not in Conformity)

  • Not material → Standard report (clean)
  • Material but NOT pervasive → Qualified opinion
  • Material AND pervasive → Adverse opinion
For GAAP departures: Qualified OR Adverse (never disclaimer).
For Scope Limitations: Qualified OR Disclaimer (never adverse).
If immaterial → always clean, regardless of type.

Explanatory Paragraphs (Clean-But Situations)

Going-Concern

Add explanatory paragraph when substantial doubt exists about entity's ability to continue. Still an unqualified opinion if F/S are properly presented. If entity doesn't disclose properly → GAAP departure → qualified or adverse.

Consistency

Add explanatory paragraph when there's a change in accounting principle (e.g., LIFO to FIFO), a change in the reporting entity form, or changes inseparable from estimate changes. May qualify if change is unjustified or not properly accounted for.

Critical Audit Matters (CAMs) — PCAOB Only

Matters communicated to the audit committee that (1) relate to material accounts/disclosures and (2) involved especially challenging, subjective, or complex auditor judgment. Required for large accelerated filers since June 2019; all issuers since December 2020. NOT required for private companies.

Group Financial Statements (Component Auditors)

When a company has multiple divisions/subsidiaries, the group auditor may engage component auditors for parts of the entity.

Group Auditor Takes Responsibility?Names Component Auditor?Report
Yes (takes full responsibility)N/AStandard unmodified opinion
No (does not take responsibility)Yes (with permission)Present component auditor's separate report
No (does not take responsibility)No (just refers to them)Indicate division of responsibility in unmodified opinion

Comparative Financial Statements

Continuing AuditorsUpdate opinion for prior periods; must be clear which opinion applies to which year's F/S
Predecessor AuditorsCan reissue their report; if not presented, current auditors' report must reference the predecessor's report and opinion on prior years

Other Information in Annual Reports

Auditors must read other information (e.g., MD&A, annual report narrative) and consider whether it's consistent with the audited F/S. No obligation to perform audit procedures over other information, but must note material inconsistencies.
